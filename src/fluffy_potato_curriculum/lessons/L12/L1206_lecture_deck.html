Slide 2.2

Say it as a translation

  • The one sentence to land: “the run_type field you set when instrumenting is the thing that decides GENERATION vs SPAN here.”
  • OpenTelemetry says “span,” Langfuse says “observation,” LangSmith says “run” — three names, one structure. You learned the structure; the names are vocabulary.
  • Exact field-name fidelity to one vendor is not the goal — approximate OTel-ish structure is. An imperfect mapping is a useful aside, not a bug.

Two ways in, same destination

What does not go in here
Extracts — the data plane. The last artifact from the intro's map, and the easiest place to get the architecture wrong.

L12 · LANGFUSE
Slide 6.1

Langfuse is observability — not your database

  • You just sent your trace to Langfuse. That is the observability plane — sampled, TTL'd, keyed by trace_id, read by you and the eval harness to answer “what did the run do?”
  • It is not where the data your agent produces should live. An extracted invoice, a scraped table, a generated record is a deliverable — the data plane — answering “what did the run make?”
  • This is the last artifact from the intro's map — extracts / new records — and the one place it's easiest to get the architecture wrong.
Both arrows cyan on purpose — doing both is the happy path.

Slide 6.2

Two calls at the boundary, two homes

  • At the point your agent produces hard data you do two independent things: record that it happened in the trace, and save the data itself to its real store. The trace holds a pointer/summary; the datastore holds the record.
  • Wiring an actual Postgres or S3 client is a data-engineering exercise — that's L20/L21 or a project brief. Today's takeaway is the boundary, not the plumbing.
